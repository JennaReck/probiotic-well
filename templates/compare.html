Refrigeration:

Probiotics that require refrigeration to keep the good bacteria alive can sometimes be less effective compared to their non-refrigerated counterparts depending on how they're shipped to stores and how they're stored afterward.

Whether refrigerated or non-refrigerated probiotics are better is still up for debate, but some people prefer the latter due to a more stable shelf life.

To get the most out of your probiotics, be sure to check the back label for refrigeration instructions. Even pill and capsule forms of probiotics often have a "refrigeration recommended" note on the back, which is easy to overlook. A brand claiming to contain 10, 25, or even 50 billion live bacteria might not mean anything as the live bacteria can die off if not properly stored.

Delivery Method:

Probiotics come in a variety of delivery methods, each with its own benefits and possible drawbacks.

Pearls:
Pearls are named such because of their small size and smooth, round shape resembling a pearl. Even someone who normally has trouble swallowing pills or tablets can probably swallow pearls with ease. Oftentimes pearls don't require refrigeration and have a coating that protects against stomach acid.

Pills:
Pressed-powder pills may or may not require refrigeration or have a coating that protects against stomach acid. Always check the label for storage recommendations.

Capsules:
As with pills, capsules may also require refrigeration. Check the label for storage recommendations.

Chewable Tablets:
Chewable probiotics are good for children or those who have trouble swallowing pills, but the downside is that they offer no protection against stomach acid which can kill the good bacteria before it has much of a chance to colonize your gut. A higher CFU (bacteria) count can better the odds of some of the good bacteria surviving, but many believe probiotics with stomach acid protection are much more effective. Chewable tablets may also require refrigeration, so be sure to check the label.

Liquids:
Liquid probiotics have much the same advantages and downsides as chewable tablets; they're easy to swallow but have no protection against stomach acid. Most liquid forms require refrigeration.

Powders:
Powder probiotics are also an option for those who have difficulty swallowing pills, but they likewise have the drawback of offering no protection against stomach acid. They may require refrigeration; check the label for storage recommendations.

Stomach Acid Protection:

In order to reach and colonize the intestines, probiotics must first make it past your acidic stomach. This is why many consider an enteric coating or some form of stomach acid protection one of the most important aspects to consider when choosing a probiotic.

Enteric coatings are usually comprised of some type of fatty acid, plant fiber, wax, plastic, or shellac.

Strains:

Bacteria are categorized by genus (or family) and species. Each species that a probiotic contains is a different strain even if it comes from the same family. Some common genuses in probiotics are lactobacillus and bifidobacterium. Each of these families contains multiple species. So a probiotic containing Lactobacillus acidophilus, Lactobacillus rhamnosus, Bifidobacterium longum, and Bifidobacterium bifidum contains 4 strains.

It's generally accepted that a probiotic which contain a bigger variety of strains is better than one that only contains 1 or 2 strains. Your gut is naturally home to dozens and dozens of different strains of 'good' bacteria, which helps it keep a healthy balance. Each different strain or species of bacteria may have a different role in your gut and help to break down or digest food in different ways. This is also why a healthy gut has such a variety of bacteria species, as should your probiotic many believe.

CFU Count:

CFU stands for colony-forming unit. It's a microbiology term for estimating viable (living) bacteria cells. So a CFU count of 1 billions means there is an estimated count of 1 billion live bacteria contained per serving for that probiotic.

CFU counts vary widely in probiotics, with 1 billion all the way up to 50 or more billion being listed. Many believe a high CFU count (perhaps 5 billion or more) to be one of the most important factors in choosing a probiotic (along with a delivery method that protects against stomach acid), but there is no set CFU count alone that makes a probiotic considered good.

The CFU count should be considered along with the storage method and whether or not it is enteric coated. A 50 billion CFU count might not be as effective as a 2 billion CFU count if it's improperly stored (not refrigerated when the label recommends it) and if most of those bacteria are killed by stomach acid (not protected by an enteric coating) before reaching the intestines.
